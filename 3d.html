3D Printing Fundamentals

Introduction to 3D Printing

Definition of additive manufacturing

The Concept: A process that builds 3D objects by adding material layer upon layer.
The Blueprint: It uses digital data from a Computer-Aided Design (CAD) model.
The Synonym: It is the industrial and umbrella term for what is commonly known as 3D printing.

Differences Between Traditional Manufacturing and 3D Printing

Feature Traditional Manufacturing (Subtractive/Formative) 3D Printing (Additive Manufacturing)
Process Cuts away material from a block or forces liquid into molds. Builds objects layer by layer from a digital model.
Material Waste High; produces significant scrap metal or plastic shavings. Minimal; uses only the exact amount of material needed.
Setup Cost Very high; requires custom molds, dies, and specialized tooling. Very low; requires only a digital file and a calibrated printer.
Production Speed Slow for prototypes, but extremely fast for millions of units. Fast for prototypes and low volumes, slow for mass production.
Design Complexity Limited; complex internal shapes or undercuts are impossible to machine. Nearly limitless; easily prints complex, hollow, or lattice geometries.
Customization Expensive; altering a product requires creating a new mold or tool. Effortless; changes are made instantly in software before printing.
Advantages
  • Low Cost: Eliminates the need for expensive custom molds or tooling setups, making single-unit production highly affordable.
  • Mass Customization: Allows every single printed part to be completely unique without increasing production costs or changing machinery.
  • Fast Production: Bypasses complex manufacturing supply chains by printing complex geometries in a single step right on site.

Types of 3D Printing Materials

Polylactic Acid (PLA)

×

Polylactic Acid (PLA)

What it is: A biodegradable thermoplastic made from renewable resources like cornstarch or sugarcane.
The Performance: Extremely easy to print, requires no heated bed, and does not emit toxic fumes, but it is brittle and warps under low heat (above 60°C).
Best used for: Visual models, rapid prototypes, hobbies, and low-stress educational projects.

Polyethylene Terephthalate Glycol

×

Polyethylene Terephthalate Glycol (PETG)

What it is: A modified version of the plastic commonly used to make clear water bottles.
The Performance: The perfect middle ground, offering the ease of printing like PLA combined with the structural strength of ABS.
Best used for: Functional prototypes, mechanical components, brackets, and outdoor parts due to its excellent weather and chemical resistance.

Acrylonitrile Butadiene Styrene (ABS)

×

Acrylonitrile Butadiene Styrene (ABS)

What it is: A tough, petroleum-based plastic famously used to manufacture LEGO bricks.

The Performance: Highly impact-resistant and heat-resistant, but prone to warping during printing and releases toxic, foul-smelling fumes that require a ventilated enclosure.

Best used for: Industrial parts, automotive components, moving gears, and items that will encounter high heat or physical abuse.

Thermoplastic Polyurethane (TPU)

×

Thermoplastic Polyurethane (TPU)

What it is: A flexible, rubber-like elastomer that can be stretched and bent without breaking.

The Performance: Highly durable and shock-absorbing, but requires a specialized extruder and very slow print speeds to prevent the soft filament from jamming.

Best used for: Phone cases, protective seals, vibration dampeners, footwear prototypes, and flexible joints.

Basic Working Principle of 3D Printing

1. Create CAD Model

The designer uses specialized computer software to build a precise digital 3D model of the desired object. This digital blueprint serves as the foundational geometry that dictates the final shape and dimensions of the physical print.

2. Export STL File

The 3D CAD model is converted into an STL file format to prepare it for manufacturing software. This process translates smooth surfaces into a mesh of tiny interlocking triangles that define the object's outer shell.

3. Slice Using Slicer Software

Slicing software cuts the digital mesh into thousands of thin horizontal layers and determines the exact machine settings. The program evaluates parameters like speed, temperature, and infill density to generate the final toolpath coordinates.

4. Send G-Code to Printer

The generated toolpath instructions are saved as G-code and transferred directly to the 3D printer's motherboard. This numerical code can be delivered via a physical SD card, a USB connection, or a wireless Wi-Fi network.

5. Printing Process Layer-by-Layer

The physical printer reads the G-code to orchestrate the precise movements of its motors, heaters, or lasers. It constructs the object from the ground up by depositing or curing material one thin layer directly on top of the last.

6. Post-Processing

Once printing concludes, the operator removes the raw part from the build plate for cleaning and refinement. This final stage involves breaking off temporary supports, sanding rough edges, or using UV ovens to fully cure the material.

Software Used for CAD Modelling

Autodesk Fusion 360

Autodesk Fusion 360 (now officially renamed Autodesk Fusion) is a cloud-based CAD, CAM, CAE, and PCB software platform. It is widely considered an industry standard for 3D printing because it handles the entire workflow—from initial concept to the final printable file—inside a single program.



CAD Modelling

CAD (Computer-Aided Design) modeling in Autodesk Fusion is built around a hybrid workspace that combines multiple design methods into one continuous workflow. It allows you to create precise, manufacturing-ready 3D models using four primary modeling techniques.
